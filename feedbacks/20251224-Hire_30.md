# Red Flag Evaluation (20251224)

**Rating**: Hire

## Question

Provide an example of a time when you had to make a difficult decision.

## Answer

In my previous role as a lead software developer at a mid-sized tech company, I encountered a significant challenge during a major product update when I discovered that a substantial portion of our legacy code was incompatible with the new features we intended to implement. Taking full ownership of the situation, I conducted a thorough risk analysis that compared two potential paths: refactoring the legacy code or maintaining the existing codebase. My analysis revealed that maintaining the existing code would likely lead to a 40% increase in technical debt and a 50% rise in future maintenance costs, which would ultimately jeopardize our product's competitiveness and frustrate our clients.

To address this issue, I synthesized my findings into a comprehensive presentation that I delivered to both the development team and senior management. In this presentation, I clearly articulated the immediate technical improvements and the strategic long-term benefits of adopting a phased refactor approach. I emphasized that this decision was crucial for resolving current technical debt and positioning our product for future growth, aligning with our clients' evolving needs.

To ensure alignment and buy-in, I proactively organized and led targeted discussions with key stakeholders, addressing their concerns and illustrating how the refactor would enhance our product‚Äôs robustness and user experience. Initially, I faced pushback from two team members who were concerned about the timeline delay and the potential impact on our quarterly goals. I addressed their concerns through one-on-one conversations, creating a detailed risk mitigation plan that specifically addressed their worries. I also involved the most resistant developer in the planning phase, which empowered them and ultimately turned them into a strong advocate for the refactor.

I proposed a phased approach to the refactor, breaking it into three phases: critical path items first, then high-impact improvements, and finally nice-to-haves. This allowed us to manage the workload effectively while minimizing disruption to our overall timeline. I implemented feature flags to enable parallel development of new features and set up a separate branch for the refactor work, merging it incrementally to facilitate testing. This approach resulted in only a 3-week delay in our release instead of the anticipated 6-8 weeks for a full refactor.

Throughout the implementation, I established clear metrics for success, including performance benchmarks and client satisfaction targets. I tracked these metrics rigorously in a dashboard that I shared with the team monthly. As a direct result of my decisive leadership in driving the phased refactoring process, we achieved a remarkable 65% improvement in page load time (from 3.2 seconds to 1.1 seconds), a 68% improvement in API response time (from 800ms to 250ms), and a 55% reduction in customer support tickets related to performance issues in the first quarter after the refactor. Additionally, we saw a 25% reduction in bug reports from clients and a 15% increase in customer satisfaction ratings.

This experience reinforced my belief in the importance of making strategic, forward-thinking decisions, even when they require difficult trade-offs. I have since integrated these lessons into my decision-making framework, ensuring that I consistently evaluate both immediate and long-term impacts in future projects. I documented my analysis and decision-making process in our team wiki and wrote a case study that highlighted my contributions, which my manager used in my performance review, ensuring that my role as the decision-maker was clear while also recognizing the efforts of my team members.
